System: origin and meanings

2025.12.28 • 4

System originates from the Greek σύστημα (sýstēma)1, which meant: whole compounded of several parts or members; composition; organized government, constitution; community, company, committee; body of soldiers, corps; a boat’s crew; machine, apparatus.

The word σύστημα compounded of parts:

  • συν- (syn-) = together
  • ἵστημι (histēmi) = causal, make to stand; set up

Today, the word system is usually used synonymously with order. However, there is another tradition that emerged in the 20th century within the scientific community. Here, a system is understood as a «whole» that is more than the sum of its parts.

For a long time, science relied on breaking things down into pieces to understand them and gain new knowledge. But at a certain point, more and more problems began to emerge that could not be solved this way.

For example, Hans Driesch found that if he grafted the future tail of a newt embryo onto where its leg would normally grow, the putative tail grew into a leg. In other words, the information determining what that part should become was contained outside of it. Therefore, to better understand a part, one must also take into account the whole of which it is a part.

Thus, alongside analysis, science began to value synthesis. Eventually, this would develop into «General Systems Theory»2.

Systems theory is still evolving, and at this moment, there is no single, universally accepted definition of a system. Here are a few of them:

  • A system is an arrangement of parts or elements that together exhibit behaviour or meaning that the individual constituents do not. (The INCOSE Fellows)

  • A system is more than the sum of its parts. It may exhibit adaptive, dynamic, goal-seeking, self-preserving, and sometimes evolutionary behavior. (Donella H. Meadows)

  • A system is a collection of parts and relationships that forms a whole that is somehow different, having its own personality as it were. (Boardman and Sauser)

Is everything a system? Most agree that not everything is. Usually, several examples are given, such as a heap (e.g., of sand) or a collection of things (that do not interact) which can be described only by listing its elements. Some even propose a checklist to verify whether what you are dealing with is a system3:

  1. Can you identify parts? … and
  2. Do the parts affect each other? … and
  3. Do the parts together produce an effect that is different from the effect of each part on its own? … and perhaps
  4. Does the effect, the behavior over time, persist in a variety of circumstances?

What is not a system is usually not discussed at length, because mistakes are easy to make here. Not seeing a system in something does not necessarily mean that there isn’t one ☺︎. That is why I prefer an approach that takes the observer into account and makes the definition more formal4.

A system consists of three related sets:

  • a set of elements
  • a set of internal interactions between elements
  • a set of external interactions between one or more elements and the external world; i.e. interactions that can be observed from outside the system.
– Erik W. Aslaksen

That is, what counts as a system is determined by the observer, by the way they describe these three sets. For example: {sun, earth}, {newtonian gravity}, {}; or {rock, scissors, paper}, {rock > scissors, scissors > paper, paper > rock}, {game rules}.

It is also worth mentioning what is arguably the most mathematical theory of systems developed by Yu. A. Urmantsev5. It is built on five basic notions: existence, a set of objects, the single, unity, and sufficiency. The definition of a system in this theory is quite complex, so I will not reproduce it here.

In summary, I support the view that a system is a concept, a mental construct, a tool that helps us make sense of reality.
